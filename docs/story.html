Story

The player has just been hired by an independent game publisher as a playtester for their upcoming narrative-adventure game, titled Common Grounds. The game is being worked on by an indie development studio made up of only six people, as well as their lead developer. Moving to a new city, the player begins their new job as the playtester for Common Grounds and builds friendships with each of their team members.

As work on Common Grounds progresses, it becomes more and more clear that the publisher funding the project, Nomina Games, is more concerned about how well the game performs than they are with the game’s quality or what it represents, and have no regard for the work-life balance of their developers either. This is made known to the public when Nomina Games is outed as an immoral and corrupt company at the Developers Conference and Showcase (DCS).

Eventually, the team (and the player) must make a choice: Continue to rely on Nomina as a reliable publisher despite conflicting values, or abandon Nomina and self-publish the game, even though this would extend development time and require each team member to take a second job in order to both fund the game and make a living. They would also lose the rights to the name Common Grounds, as the name is owned by Nomina.

If the player chooses to stay with Nomina, they will finish the game in undesirable working conditions and their vision will be corrupted by executive meddling. When the game releases a month later, fans and critics are divided on whether or not it is actually good, but it still ends up being financially successful. The team becomes an in-house developer for Nomina and is immediately assigned to work on a sequel, despite suffering from burnout and a lack of ideas. This will be called “The Lost” ending.

If the player chooses to cut ties with Nomina, they will lose all funding for the project as well as the naming rights, but can now self-publish the game and no longer have to endure the poor working environment. The team can now work at their own pace and use whatever ideas they want. However, they are no longer backed by a publisher, so each team member must take a second job in order to keep the project funded. This caused development to take another whole year, but working on the game became significantly more enjoyable and the team’s relationships with one another strengthened as a result. When the game does eventually release, it does so to moderate success and a small but adoring audience. It is also given a new title: Everyday. This will be called “The Wanderer” ending.
